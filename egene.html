eGene Study

Behavioral and environmental factors associated with cancer risk by pathogenic variant status. This project examines how lifestyle, medication, medical history, and environmental exposures relate to cancer risk in a hereditary-cancer-enriched cohort.

Overview

The eGene Study is a participant-reported cohort designed to explore whether modifiable exposures and clinical history are associated with cancer risk in people with and without pathogenic variants. The central question is whether these associations differ by genetic background, especially in individuals already at elevated hereditary risk.

In the main analysis, the study evaluated demographic, reproductive, medication, lifestyle, medical history, and environmental variables, then tested their association with overall cancer risk and selected cancer subgroups.

Study Design

Study setup

This was a cross-sectional analysis of survey data collected through the Eureka digital platform between 2020 and 2025. Participants were recruited through multiple channels, including EHR outreach, email campaigns, newsletters, and clinic-based recruitment.

Data collection

The questionnaire covered demographics, reproductive factors, medication use, lifestyle factors, medical history, and environmental exposures. Genetic testing status was self-reported and used to define pathogenic variant carrier status.

Preprocessing

Survey versions were harmonized, free-text responses were categorized with a Python-based workflow, and selected continuous variables were grouped for interpretation and model stability.

Methods

  • Descriptive statistics for participant characteristics.
  • Univariate logistic regression to screen exposures by PV status.
  • LASSO for feature selection and collinearity reduction.
  • Stability-based selection repeated multiple times.
  • Multivariable logistic regression with subgroup analyses.
  • Breast cancer and prostate cancer subgroup models.
R was used for the statistical modeling, while Python was used for preprocessing and free-text classification.

Key Findings

Overall cancer risk

Several exposures were associated with cancer risk in the full cohort, including hepatitis B vaccination in a protective direction and thyroid medication, smallpox vaccination, and steroid use in a positive direction.

PV carrier subgroup

Some associations remained in PV carriers, and vitamin D supplementation was significant only in this subgroup. Environmental exposures also appeared more heterogeneous by carrier status.

Breast cancer analysis

In women, PV carrier status remained strongly associated with breast cancer, and meat consumption, selected vaccinations, and non-cancer radiation exposure showed additional signals.

Prostate cancer analysis

In men, vitamin D supplementation and family cancer history were associated with prostate cancer risk in the subgroup analysis.

Limitations

  • Self-reported exposures and outcomes may introduce recall bias.
  • Non-random participation may limit generalizability.
  • Missing genetic testing results reduce coverage of PV status.
  • Some subgroup estimates are imprecise because of small sample sizes.

These points align with the limitations discussed in the manuscript.

Next Steps

Future work can extend this project with larger cohorts, prospective follow-up, and more objective exposure measurement. That would make the page feel complete while leaving room for the eventual manuscript PDF or conference poster.
